## It Starts at the Fibre

Long before a garment is cut, its fate is decided by the fibre. Long-staple cottons, fine merino, and properly retted linen produce yarn that is stronger, softer, and far more durable than their short-staple equivalents. You can feel the difference in seconds and live with it for years.

## Weight and Weave

A good fabric has integrity in the hand — it falls rather than floats, and it recovers when you crush it in a fist. Hold a cheap shirt to the light and you'll often see straight through it; the weave is loose to save on yarn. Quality cloth is dense without being heavy, and that density is what lets it survive hundreds of washes.

## The Finishing Tells the Truth

Much of what people read as "cheap" is actually poor finishing: pilling, twisting at the seams, colour that bleeds or fades after a few washes. Proper finishing — pre-shrinking, careful dyeing, settled seams — costs more and shows less, which is exactly why corners get cut there first.

## What to Look For

- Tightly woven cloth that doesn't show light through it
- A hand that feels substantial but still drapes
- Even, flat seams with generous allowance
- Natural fibres, or honest blends with a clear purpose

Spend your attention here and the rest of the garment usually follows. Fabric is not a detail — it is the product.
