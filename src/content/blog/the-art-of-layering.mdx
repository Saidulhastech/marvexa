## Start with the Base

Good layering begins closest to the skin. A fine-gauge cotton tee or a lightweight merino long-sleeve sits flat under everything else and regulates temperature without bulk. The base layer is the one most people skimp on — and the one that quietly decides whether the whole outfit reads sharp or sloppy.

Keep base layers in your core neutrals so they disappear under, or peek out from, every other piece you own.

## Build in Odd Numbers

Three visible layers almost always look more considered than two. A tee, an open shirt, and a jacket. A knit, a blazer, and a coat. The eye reads odd-numbered groupings as intentional rather than accidental — a small trick borrowed from interior styling that works just as well on the body.

## Play with Proportion and Weight

Layering isn't only about warmth; it's about contrast. Pair something fitted with something relaxed, something matte with something with a little sheen. A chunky knit over a crisp shirt collar. A structured coat over soft, draping trousers. Contrast is what keeps a layered look from flattening into a single shapeless block.

## Let Fabric Do the Work

Natural fibres layer best because they breathe. Linen, cotton, and wool move air rather than trapping it, so you can add a layer without overheating the moment you step indoors. Reach for these first when the forecast can't make up its mind.
