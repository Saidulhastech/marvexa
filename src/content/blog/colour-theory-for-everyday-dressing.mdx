## Tone Before Colour

Before worrying about which colours go together, think about tone — how light or dark a colour reads. Outfits built on a deliberate tonal relationship look considered even when the hues are simple. A head-to-toe range of soft stones and creams works because the tones are in conversation.

## Build Around Neutrals

Neutrals are the grammar of a wardrobe; colour is the punctuation. Anchor most of an outfit in navy, charcoal, cream, or tan, then let a single considered colour do the talking. One accent reads as intent. Three competing accents read as noise.

## Use Contrast Deliberately

High contrast — cream against navy — feels crisp and graphic. Low contrast — stone against camel — feels calm and refined. Neither is better; they set different moods. Decide which you want before you get dressed, and let the contrast level carry it.

## Find Your Accent

Most people suit a small family of accent colours — often a muted version of something they're naturally drawn to. Sage, rust, ochre, and slate flatter far more people than primary brights. Test an accent near your face in daylight: the right one makes you look rested, the wrong one makes the garment the loudest thing in the room.
